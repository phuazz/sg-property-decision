Enter a few details and see the biggest loan you can take, the monthly repayment, and the cash you need upfront. The one that catches people out: your age quietly shrinks the loan, because the best terms need it repaid by 65.

How the numbers are worked out
The engine applies the published rules — the loan-to-value cap (LTV), the Total Debt Servicing Ratio tested at a 4% stress rate (TDSR), and the Mortgage Servicing Ratio for HDB/EC (MSR) — and returns the biggest loan you can take, the instalment at both the stress rate and the real package rate, and the full upfront cash bill (downpayment, buyer's stamp duty, and ABSD where it applies). It is an illustration of the rules, not a bank's approval.

Pay cash for peace of mind, or borrow cheaply?

With interest rates near 1%, the loan costs less than what your cash could earn elsewhere — so on the maths, borrowing and keeping cash invested usually wins. But "no mortgage, less stress" is real too. This weighs both, and the safety buffer that matters more than either.

Why cheap debt changes the answer
3-month SORA is near 1% and packages are around 1.3–1.7% — below both property's long-run return and a balanced portfolio's. So the expected-value answer is to borrow and keep cash invested, provided you hold enough liquid reserve never to be a forced seller. This makes the trade-off explicit; it does not pretend the future is known.

The forced-seller buffer

The one rule that keeps leverage safe: hold enough liquid cash to cover the instalment through a spell of unemployment or a rate spike, so a bad market never forces a sale. The brief set this at 2–3 years.

Prepay versus stay invested

HDB, condo or landed — which suits you?

Each strikes a different balance between price, how easily you can sell, rental yield, how much you can borrow, and the rules. For a home you want to hold yet still sell easily, bought with cheap leverage, a resale condo tends to fit — but the scorecard lays it out so the choice is yours.

Where to buy — and what the land bids are telling you

Where your budget actually reaches, what each area yields, and a forward signal most buyers miss: developers' winning land bids. A high, competitive bid prices tomorrow's launches and sets a floor under nearby prices.

How to read a land bid
Government Land Sales are how the state releases sites; developers tender for them. The winning $psf per plot ratio (ppr) is what a developer paid per square foot of sellable space — so the eventual launch price is roughly that bid × 1.8–2.0 (to cover construction, financing and margin). A high bid with many bidders near an area you like is a forward vote of confidence that pulls nearby resale prices up; a lone low bid is the opposite.

Segments — price, yield, momentum

Median $psf and gross yields are segment aggregates (several portal-sourced, flagged in the data); a specific project can differ materially. Momentum is the latest quarterly q/q (official from data.gov.sg where live, else the Q2-26 flash). Gross yield is before maintenance, tax and vacancy — net is typically ~0.8–1.2pp lower.
