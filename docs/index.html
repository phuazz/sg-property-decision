How the numbers are worked out
The engine applies the published rules — the loan-to-value cap (LTV), the Total Debt Servicing Ratio tested at the higher of the 4% floor or your actual rate (TDSR), and the Mortgage Servicing Ratio for HDB/EC (MSR, same floor; 3% for HDB loans) — and returns the biggest loan you can take, the instalment at both the stress rate and the real package rate, and the full upfront cash bill (downpayment, buyer's stamp duty, and ABSD where it applies). Variable and rental income is counted at 70%. It is an illustration of the rules, not a bank's approval.

Upfront cash — where every dollar goes
The downpayment splits into a minimum cash portion and a CPF-or-cash portion (an HDB loan needs no minimum cash). BSD is the buyer's stamp duty; ABSD is nil for a citizen's first property. Legal and valuation are estimated at ~S$4,000. Buying a new launch? Payment is progressive — BSD falls due within 14 days of exercising the option, and instalments ramp with construction rather than starting in full. Paying above valuation? That gap is extra cash on top of everything here.

Pay cash for peace of mind, or borrow cheaply?

With interest rates near 1%, the loan costs less than what your cash could earn elsewhere — so on the maths, borrowing and keeping cash invested usually wins. But "no mortgage, less stress" is real too. This weighs both, and the safety buffer that matters more than either.

Why cheap debt changes the answer
3-month SORA is near 1% and packages are around 1.3–1.7% — below both property's long-run return and a balanced portfolio's. So the expected-value answer is to borrow and keep cash invested, provided you hold enough liquid reserve never to be a forced seller. This makes the trade-off explicit; it does not pretend the future is known. One caveat: fixed packages typically reprice after 2–3 years — the stress instalment on the Affordability tab is the guard for exactly that.

The forced-seller buffer — and the exit lock

The one rule that keeps leverage safe: hold enough liquid reserve to cover the instalment through a spell of unemployment or a rate spike, so a bad market never forces a sale. The brief set this at 2–3 years. CPF can pay instalments, but maintenance, tax and living costs need actual cash.

HDB, condo or landed — which suits you?

Each strikes a different balance between price, how easily you can sell, rental yield, how much you can borrow, and the rules. For a home you want to hold yet still sell easily, bought with cheap leverage, a resale condo tends to fit — but the scorecard lays it out so the choice is yours.

Where to buy — and what the land bids are telling you

Where your budget actually reaches, what each area yields, and a forward signal most buyers miss: developers' winning land bids. A high, competitive bid prices tomorrow's launches and sets a floor under nearby prices.

How to read a land bid
Government Land Sales are how the state releases sites; developers tender for them. The winning $psf per plot ratio (ppr) is what a developer paid per square foot of sellable space — so the eventual launch price is roughly that bid × 1.8–2.0 (to cover construction, financing and margin). A high bid with many bidders near an area you like is a forward vote of confidence that pulls nearby resale prices up; a lone low bid is the opposite.

Segments — price, yield, momentum

Median $psf and gross yields are segment aggregates (several portal-sourced, flagged in the data); a specific project can differ materially. Momentum is the latest quarterly q/q (official from data.gov.sg where live, else the Q2-26 flash). Gross yield is before maintenance, tax and vacancy — net is typically ~0.8–1.2pp lower.
